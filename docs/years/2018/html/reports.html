Preface

Several of you (legitimately) asked some specifics about the final report, like whether this piece of information should be included in the report or in more general what's exactly expected to be in the report. They are all legitimate questions and I may have been too vague about this, but honestly I've never thought about instructing you with much specifics about the exact contents, style, format or structure, at least in superficial terms.

With that said, let me try my best to be clear about what I am generally expecting in the final report, with some minimum (superficial) requirements such as the length.

Below, I first address what I expect for the Option 1 (Optimization of VGG), as this case is easier to explain specifically.

(Superficial) requirements for "Optimization of VGG" report

  • A report must be a sufficiently self-contained document that provides the reader with information about what you did. It's not like the report of the SpMV hands-on;

    in that SpMV hands-on, I, essentially, asked you to paste only execution logs, with a very detailed instruction on what you should do in each step. One reason was that the main goal was exposing you to parallel computing. I just wanted to bring everybody on the same page about how you do OpenMP, how you do CUDA, how to launch jobs, etc. That's why I gave detailed instructions on what you are supposed to do. The other reason was I wanted to minimize your work other than coding.

    What I expect in the final report is different. It is exactly what you thought you should do. I did not (do not want to) give you details on what you should do and how you should do it. I gave lots of hints about where you should get started, but in the end of the day, it is you who should think about and decide what to do. Therefore, the most basic information I expect from you in the final report is what you thought you should do to accomplish the goal and what you actually did. Describe your thought and the action you took in a convincing manner.

  • The number of pages. Well, again, I do not want to be too specific, as the number of pages is something you can easily play with. There can be a report of 5 pages having much denser information than a report of 20 pages whose most contents are copy of unimportant part of code or unimportant part of the execution logs.

    With that said, I would say I would raise eyebrows if I receive a report of two or three pages. I believe that any serious description of any serious effort on this problem won't fit three pages.

  • If you worry about what to write to make it not too thin, you may be (wrongly) thinking it's just about inserting a few #omp pragma parallel for's (or straightforwardly parallelizing a few loops with CUDA). Just think about the maximum performance the machine is hopefully able to achieve (e.g., maximum FLOPS, the maximum memory bandwidth or any other limitation of the machine that applies to the specific code you are trying to optimize) and assess how close your code is to the maximum. I don't say they should be very close but if they are too far, there are much left to do and this is where your serious effort should begin. In addition, this demands an analysis of your code (why it performance only so well). Even if it is only about parallelization, chances are that you are unlikely to get a linear speedup (Px speedup with P cores) in the initial attempt. This is another place where your analysis will start.

    There will be a lot to write about how you understand and analyze the performance.

  • For a team report, the report can be two separate reports or a combined single report. Either way, each individual should submit a report (in the latter case, two members submit the same report). For a combined report,
    • it should be a single coherent document; it should not be a document just stapling otherwise two separate documents.
    • how two members worked must be clarified in a separate section, perhaps in an appendix (e.g., who did which part of the experiments, who wrote which section, etc.)
